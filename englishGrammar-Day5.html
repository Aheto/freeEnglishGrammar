📘 Master Class: Sentence Formation & Review

Welcome to Day 5! Today we are putting everything together. We will learn the strict rules for forming grammatically correct statements and questions from groups of words. We will also look ahead to how other verbs (besides "to be") are formed in the Simple Present tense. Let's review and master sentence formation!

📝 Part 1: Word Order and Sentence Formation

To form grammatically correct statements or questions, proper word order is essential. When you are given a group of jumbled words, you must look for specific clues to determine the type of sentence:

Deep Dive:
• If the symbol "?" is present, you must form the words into a question. The verb must precede the subject.
• If the word "not" is present, you must form the words into a negative statement or a negative question.
• Important Rule: Do not use contractions when practicing strict formal sentence formation. Always write out the full words (e.g., use "Are they not ready?" instead of "Aren't they ready?").

🧩 Part 2: Practice with Examples

Let's look at how to apply these rules using the verb "to be" in the Simple Present tense:

Examples:
• Words: "it, brown" $\rightarrow$ Statement: "It is brown."
• Words: "you, ?, excited" $\rightarrow$ Question: "Are you excited?" (Notice the "?" means the verb "Are" comes first).
• Words: "I, satisfied, not" $\rightarrow$ Negative Statement: "I am not satisfied."
• Words: "no, ?, they, ready" $\rightarrow$ Negative Question: "Are they not ready?" (Notice "no" translates to "not" in formal English grammar).

📖 Part 3: Introduction to Other Verb Forms

So far, we have focused entirely on the verb "to be". But what about all other verbs like "walk", "run", or "eat"? The Simple Present of any verb other than the verb "to be" is formed from the Bare Infinitive of the verb.

Deep Dive: As we learned on Day 1, the bare infinitive consists of the infinitive without the word "to". This is the exact form in which English verbs are usually listed in dictionaries.
Real-Life Example: If you look up "walking" in a dictionary, you won't find it. You will find the bare infinitive: "walk". To use it in the Simple Present, you just add "s" or "es" for third-person singular subjects (e.g., "He walks", "She eats").
💭 Think About It: If you are given the words "we, ?, no, correct", how would you form a formal negative question without using contractions? (Answer: "Are we not correct?"). Why is it important to know the bare infinitive when learning new verbs?
