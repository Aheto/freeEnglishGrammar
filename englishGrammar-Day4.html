📘 Master Class: Contractions & Tag Questions

Welcome to Day 4! Today we are going to explore how English speakers shorten words in everyday conversation using contractions, learn the tricky rule for "I am not", and discover how to add tag questions to the end of our sentences to check for agreement. Let's dive in!

🗣️ Part 1: Contractions in Spoken English

In spoken English, contractions are often used in negative questions. In the contracted form of a negative question, the contraction of "not" follows immediately after the verb.

Deep Dive: Notice how the verb and "not" merge together using an apostrophe.
• Are you not awake? → Aren't you awake?
• Is he not awake? → Isn't he awake?
• Are we not awake? → Aren't we awake?
Real-Life Example: When you are surprised your friend is up early, you might ask, "Aren't you awake yet?" instead of the more formal "Are you not awake yet?"

⚠️ Part 2: The Special Rule for "I am not"

There is no universally accepted contraction for "I am not". In spoken English, "I am not" is often contracted to "aren't I". However, although the expression "aren't I" is considered acceptable in informal English, it is not considered to be grammatically correct in formal English. In formal English, no contraction should be used for "I am not".

Deep Dive:
• Informal/Spoken: "Aren't I awake?" (Acceptable in conversation)
• Formal/Written: "Am I not awake?" (Grammatically correct)
Real-Life Example: If you are writing a formal essay, never write "Aren't I". Always write "Am I not". But when chatting with friends, "Aren't I coming with you?" is perfectly fine!

🏷️ Part 3: Tag Questions

A tag question is a question added at the end of a sentence. A tag question following an affirmative statement generally has the form of a negative question, with the meaning: "isn't it so?". In English, tag questions vary, depending on the verbs and subjects of the preceding statements. The subjects and verbs of the preceding statements are repeated in the tag question.

Deep Dive: Contractions are usually used in negative tag questions in spoken English.
• I am awake. → I am awake, am I not? (No formal contraction for 'I am not')
• You are awake. → You are awake, aren't you?
• She is awake. → She is awake, isn't she?
• We are awake. → We are awake, aren't we?
• They are awake. → They are awake, aren't they?
Real-Life Example: You look out the window at the dark clouds and say, "It is going to rain, isn't it?" You are stating a fact and asking for confirmation!
💭 Think About It: If you want to confirm that your friend is hungry, how would you use a tag question? (e.g., "You are hungry, aren't you?"). Why wouldn't you say "You are hungry, aren't I?"
