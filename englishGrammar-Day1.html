📘 Master Class: Parts of Speech & Verb Functions

Welcome to Day 1! Today we are going to explore how words in the English language perform different functions depending on how they are used. We will look at how the same word can be a noun or a verb, understand how verbs change form based on time (tenses), and learn about the infinitive form of verbs. Let's get started!

🏷️ Part 1: Functions of Words (Nouns and Verbs)

Words in English perform different functions. Nouns are words that perform the function of naming things. Verbs are words that perform the function of expressing states or actions. Many English words can perform more than one function depending on the context!

Deep Dive: Let's look at the word "comb".
• Noun: "I have lost my comb." (Naming a thing)
• Verb: "I comb my hair every morning." (Expressing an action)
Real-Life Example: Similarly, "water" can be a noun ("Water is a necessity of life") or a verb ("Do you water your plants once a week?").

⏳ Part 2: Verb Forms and Tenses

English verbs may have different forms depending on the subject of the verb and when the action takes place. These different verb forms are called tenses.

Deep Dive:
• Simple Present Tense: Used to express actions taking place in the present or habits. Example: "He always walks to work."
• Simple Past Tense: Used to express actions which took place in the past. Example: "He walked to work yesterday."
Real-Life Example: Notice how the verb changes from "walks" to "walked" just by changing the time from present to past! Also, notice how "We live" changes to "He lives" based on the subject.

📖 Part 3: The Infinitive Form

The infinitive form of a verb is its base form. In English, the infinitive begins with the word "to" (e.g., "to walk"). The bare infinitive consists of the infinitive without the word "to". This is the form in which English verbs are usually listed in dictionaries.

Deep Dive: If you look up a verb in the dictionary, you won't find "walks" or "walked". You will find the bare infinitive: "walk".
Real-Life Example: "To walk" is the infinitive. "Walk" is the bare infinitive. "Walking" and "walked" are different tense forms derived from the bare infinitive.
💭 Think About It: If you were writing a dictionary entry for the word "play", what would the bare infinitive be? How would you use "play" as a noun in a sentence? (e.g., "We went to see a play at the theatre.")
